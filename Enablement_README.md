# Enablement

Enablement is where most AI programmes underinvest and then wonder why adoption is low. A tool no one knows how to use confidently will not get used — or it will get used badly, which is often worse. The root cause is almost always the same: the programme was designed around the technology, not around the people who need to change their behaviour to make it work.

This is pure change management territory. Knowledge transfer without behaviour change is training theatre. The goal is not for people to attend a session — it's for them to work differently six months later.

---

## Framework 1: The AI Capability Maturity Model

A five-level model for assessing where individuals, teams, and the organisation sit in their AI capability journey. Knowing where people actually are — not where the programme assumes they are — determines what kind of intervention will land.

**Level 1 — Unaware:** Little to no understanding of what AI can do or why it's relevant to their role. The gap here is Awareness (ADKAR stage 1).

**Level 2 — Aware:** Understands AI at a conceptual level but hasn't applied it. The gap is Desire (ADKAR stage 2) — they know it exists but don't see why they should engage.

**Level 3 — Experimenting:** Actively trying AI tools but in an ad-hoc, unstructured way. The gap is Knowledge (ADKAR stage 3) — they need structured guidance on how to apply AI effectively in their context.

**Level 4 — Practising:** Using AI consistently and productively in their role. The gap shifts to Ability (ADKAR stage 4) — deepening skill and building judgment about when to use AI and when not to.

**Level 5 — Championing:** Driving AI adoption in their team, identifying new use cases, and helping others progress. These are your change agents. Activate them.

Run a maturity assessment before designing any enablement intervention. Delivering a Level 3 programme to a Level 1 audience accelerates nobody.

## Framework 2: Role-Based Learning Pathways

Generic AI training is wasted. A finance analyst and a software engineer share almost no AI learning needs beyond the basics. Role-based pathways build the specific skills that translate directly into changed behaviour in each function.

Three layers per role:

**Foundation:** What every employee needs — AI literacy, responsible use principles, how to evaluate an AI output critically. This is not optional and should not be treated as optional.

**Functional:** The specific AI applications and workflows relevant to that role. Built with input from people actually doing the job, not designed in isolation by L&D.

**Advanced:** For those who want to build, configure, or lead AI initiatives within their domain. The pipeline for your internal champions.

**Change management lens:** Role-based pathways send a signal beyond the content. They communicate that the organisation sees people as individuals with specific contexts, not as a uniform mass to push through a generic programme. That perception difference materially affects engagement.

## How to Apply

**Run the maturity assessment as a listening exercise, not just a diagnostic.** The conversations you have while assessing maturity levels will surface resistance, concerns, and use case ideas that no survey will capture. Use it to build relationships with frontline teams before the formal programme begins.

**Identify and invest in your Level 5 champions early.** These people exist in every organisation — the ones who are already exploring AI on their own, sharing tips with colleagues, asking harder questions. Find them, give them air cover, and build them into your change network. They will do more for adoption than any top-down mandate.

**Measure behaviour change, not training completion.** Completion rates are a vanity metric. The measure that matters is whether people are working differently — using AI outputs in their decisions, spending less time on tasks that AI now handles, escalating appropriately when AI produces something they don't trust. Design measurement into the programme from the start.

**Reinforce continuously, not just at launch.** The R in ADKAR — Reinforcement — is the most neglected stage. New behaviours need repeated reinforcement over months to become default. Build in follow-up touchpoints: team huddles, peer showcases, monthly "what's working" check-ins. The launch event is the beginning of the change, not the end.
