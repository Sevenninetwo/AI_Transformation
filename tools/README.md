# Tools

Tool selection is the decision that gets made too fast and regretted too slowly. A vendor demo in a conference room, a proof of concept with clean data, and an enthusiastic sponsor can produce a purchasing decision that takes years and significant political capital to undo.

The technology question and the people question need to be answered together. A tool that the right people do not trust, do not understand, or were not consulted on selecting will underperform a technically inferior tool that was co-designed with its users. Change management is not a post-purchase activity — it starts at evaluation.

---

## Framework 1: The Build vs. Buy vs. Partner Decision Model

Three routes to AI capability, each with a distinct risk profile and change footprint.

| Route | Best When | Key Risk |
|---|---|---|
| Build | Use case is core to competitive differentiation, data is too sensitive to move outside, no market solution exists | Slower initial delivery, requires sustained internal technical investment |
| Buy | Use case is non-differentiating, mature market solutions exist, speed to value outweighs control | Vendor dependency, limited control over model internals, data residency considerations |
| Partner | Internal capability gaps are real but temporary, engagement is structured to build internal ownership | Knowledge stays with the partner if transfer is not built into the contract |

**Change management lens:** The decision affects more than the technology. A Build decision signals investment in internal capability and careers. A Buy decision can trigger anxiety in technical teams who worry about redundancy. Being transparent about the rationale — and what it means for people — is not optional.

---

## Framework 2: The AI Tool Evaluation Scorecard

A structured evaluation model that prevents decisions from being made on demo quality alone. Score each candidate tool across six dimensions before a procurement conversation begins.

| Dimension | Score (0-5) | What to Assess |
|---|---|---|
| Capability Fit | /5 | Does the tool do what the use case requires, at the scale required? |
| Data Compatibility | /5 | Does it work with existing data infrastructure without significant re-engineering? |
| Security and Compliance | /5 | Does it meet data residency, privacy, and regulatory requirements? |
| Integration Complexity | /5 | How much engineering effort is required to connect to existing systems? |
| Adoption Readiness | /5 | How intuitive is it for intended users? What does vendor enablement look like? |
| Vendor Viability | /5 | Is this vendor likely to exist and invest in this product in three years? |

**Change management lens:** Include intended end-users in the evaluation process. A representative group doing structured testing on candidate tools — not just watching a demo — will surface usability issues early, build familiarity with the eventual winner, and generate internal advocates who can support rollout.

## How to Apply

**Make the Build/Buy/Partner decision at the use case level, not the programme level.** Different use cases warrant different routes. A blanket "we build everything" or "we buy everything" policy is rarely the right answer across a diverse portfolio.

**Score the evaluation before talking to vendors.** Establish the weighting of each scorecard dimension internally first. The moment vendor conversations start, there is pressure — subtle or explicit — to adjust criteria in favour of what is available. Anchoring criteria in advance protects the integrity of the decision.

**Structure vendor pilots around real work, not showcase scenarios.** Give shortlisted vendors access to a representative sample of real data and a real use case. Pilot performance with clean synthetic data tells you almost nothing about production performance.

**Plan the change footprint alongside the technical rollout.** Before any tool goes to production, answer three questions: Who needs to change their behaviour to use this? What does that behaviour change require from them? What happens if they do not change? The answers determine the enablement and communication plan — and they need to be answered before go-live, not after.
