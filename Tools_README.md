# Tools

Tool selection is the decision that gets made too fast and regretted too slowly. A vendor demo in a conference room, a proof of concept with clean data, and an enthusiastic sponsor can produce a purchasing decision that takes years and significant political capital to undo.

The technology question and the people question need to be answered together. A tool that the right people don't trust, don't understand, or weren't consulted on selecting will underperform a technically inferior tool that was co-designed with its users. Change management is not a post-purchase activity — it starts at evaluation.

---

## Framework 1: The Build vs. Buy vs. Partner Decision Model

Three routes to AI capability, each with a distinct risk profile and change footprint.

**Build:** Develop AI capability in-house. Maximum control over data, model behaviour, and iteration speed. Requires a technical team, a longer runway, and the organisational patience to accept slower initial delivery in exchange for long-term ownership.
*Best when:* The use case is core to competitive differentiation, data is too sensitive to move outside the organisation, or no market solution exists.

**Buy:** Purchase a third-party AI product or platform. Faster time to value, lower technical overhead, vendor handles model maintenance. Less control over the model's internals, dependency on vendor roadmap, and data residency considerations.
*Best when:* The use case is non-differentiating, the market has mature solutions, and speed to value outweighs control.

**Partner:** Co-develop with an external partner — a consultancy, systems integrator, or AI lab. Combines external expertise with internal context. The risk is knowledge transfer: if the internal capability doesn't grow during the engagement, the organisation is dependent the moment the partner leaves.
*Best when:* Internal capability gaps are real but temporary, and the engagement is structured to build internal ownership over time.

**Change management lens:** The decision affects more than the technology. A "Build" decision signals investment in internal capability and careers. A "Buy" decision can trigger anxiety in technical teams who worry about redundancy. Being transparent about the rationale — and what it means for people — is not optional.

## Framework 2: The AI Tool Evaluation Scorecard

A structured evaluation model that prevents decisions from being made on demo quality alone. Score each candidate tool across six dimensions before a procurement conversation begins.

**Capability Fit (0–5):** Does the tool do what the use case requires, at the scale required?

**Data Compatibility (0–5):** Does it work with the organisation's existing data infrastructure without requiring significant re-engineering?

**Security & Compliance (0–5):** Does it meet the organisation's data residency, privacy, and regulatory requirements? Non-negotiable criteria should be marked as knockout factors, not just lower scores.

**Integration Complexity (0–5):** How much engineering effort is required to connect this tool to existing systems? A high-capability tool with a painful integration will delay value and drain goodwill.

**Adoption Readiness (0–5):** How intuitive is the tool for its intended users? What does the vendor's enablement offering look like? A tool that requires six weeks of training before anyone can use it has a different change management cost than one that gets to value in a week.

**Vendor Viability (0–5):** Is this vendor likely to exist and invest in this product in three years? The AI tooling market is consolidating fast. Choosing a vendor with a weak balance sheet or a product that a larger player will absorb is a real risk.

**Change management lens:** Include intended end-users in the evaluation process. A representative group doing structured testing on candidate tools — not just watching a demo — will surface usability issues early, build familiarity with the eventual winner, and generate internal advocates who can support rollout.

## How to Apply

**Make the Build/Buy/Partner decision at the use case level, not the programme level.** Different use cases warrant different routes. A blanket "we build everything" or "we buy everything" policy is rarely the right answer across a diverse portfolio.

**Score the evaluation before talking to vendors.** Establish the weighting of each scorecard dimension internally first. The moment vendor conversations start, there is pressure — subtle or explicit — to adjust criteria in favour of what's available. Anchoring criteria in advance protects the integrity of the decision.

**Structure vendor pilots around real work, not showcase scenarios.** Give shortlisted vendors access to a representative sample of real data and a real use case. Pilot performance in controlled conditions with clean synthetic data tells you almost nothing about production performance.

**Plan the change footprint alongside the technical rollout.** Before any tool goes to production, answer three questions: Who needs to change their behaviour to use this? What does that behaviour change require from them? What happens if they don't change? The answers determine the enablement and communication plan, and they need to be answered before go-live, not after.
