# AI Transformation Leadership

This repo documents my approach to leading AI transformation inside organisations. It is structured across five pillars that I have found to be the load-bearing walls of any serious transformation: **Strategy, Data, Governance, Enablement, and Tools**.

Each section contains the frameworks I actually use and a practical guide on how to apply them. Change management is not a separate workstream here - it is embedded in how every pillar is executed. 

Technology without behaviour change is just expensive software.

## Change Management Reflections 

AI transformation fails more often at the people layer than the technology layer. I have seen organisations deploy sophisticated models into workflows where nobody trusts them, nobody was consulted, and nobody knows what to do when they produce a wrong output.

The frameworks across these five pillars are shaped by a core principle from change management: **people support what they help create.** That means involving the right stakeholders early, communicating the why before the what, celebrating early wins loudly enough to shift sceptics, and reinforcing new behaviours until they become the default — not the exception.

Two models I return to consistently:

**ADKAR** (Prosci) — ensures individuals move through Awareness, Desire, Knowledge, Ability, and Reinforcement. Most AI programs stall at Knowledge. They train people but never build the Ability or Reinforcement to make new behaviours stick. Remember that workshop you attended for half a day with no follow-up afterwards? That is what I am talking about. 

**Kotter's 8-Step Model** — particularly useful for the early phases. Creating urgency, building a guiding coalition, and anchoring change in culture are not soft activities. They are the structural conditions that determine whether an AI programme scales or stagnates. It's mandatory. 

Every pillar section references where these principles apply in practice.

## A Note on AI Playbooks

There is no universal AI playbook. Any consultant or vendor who hands you one should be treated with scepticism.

Every company carries a different legacy: a different data estate, a different culture, a different risk appetite, a different competitive clock speed. What works at a 10,000-person financial institution will not translate cleanly to a 200-person SaaS company. The frameworks here are starting points, not scripts.

What does hold across every context is this: **lasting AI transformation requires a culture that moves in both directions simultaneously** — leadership setting the strategic direction and creating the conditions, and practitioners on the ground surfacing what is actually working. Top-down without bottom-up produces mandates nobody follows. Bottom-up without top-down produces a fragmented collection of pilots that never scale.

The companies that get this right do not just implement AI. They build the internal muscle to keep adapting as the technology evolves — because it will keep evolving faster than any roadmap can anticipate.
